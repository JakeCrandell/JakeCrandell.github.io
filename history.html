History of Scorekeeping

The First Box Scores

Back in the late 1850s, baseball was really gaining a lot of fans, especially in the east coast. One of the biggest series back then was between the All Brooklyn team and the All New York team. In this time, they just played the game, that was pretty much it. Then they came up with basically two statistics to keep track of: Hands Lost and Runs. Both these statistics were what we would consider to be an offensive statistic, or batting statistic. Hands Lost was just how many times you got out while Runs was how many times you scored. This was also when the first box scores were just starting to make it into local newspapers. The first couple statistics listed in a box score in the newspaper was one of the main ways people found out about this interesting sport called baseball.

Henry Chadwick

One of the interested, curious attendees of some of those early baseball games between New York and Brooklyn was a man named Henry Chadwick. Chadwick attended those games barely knowing anything about baseball, but he left those games with a new interest that would quickly become his life long passion. Chadwick started analyzing every part of the game of baseball. He started coming up with new ways to judge a player's skill, leading to brand new statistics never seen before. Chadwick had, and still has, so much meaning in the world of baseball. He later became known as "The Father of Baseball". It is just amazing that baseball started out with only two statistics, which were very close in nature to begin with, but then because of men like Henry Chadwick, we now have hundreds of possible ways to view a single game.

Scorekeeping Drives Baseball

In today's world, if you go to an afternoon baseball game, you won't see many people, if any at all, with pencil and paper trying to physically keep score of the game. That is because in today's stadiums there are huge scoreboards that tell you a lot of what you need instantaneously. Well, back when baseball was first getting popular, the only way to see how a certain player was playing that day was to physically keep score for yourself. This new found scorekeeping took on a lifeform of its own. Pretty soon everybody was bringing their pencil and paper to the stadium with them. Scorekeeping played a huge role in the popularity of baseball.
